Art of Storytelling

A complete guide to foreshadowing, conflict, premise, character, plot, and environment — the building blocks of every great story.

↓ scroll

01 — Concept

Foreshadowing

Foreshadowing is a literary technique that promises

⚡

Tension

Builds emotional unease and anticipation. The audience senses danger before it arrives, making the payoff more powerful.

🎭

Dramatic Irony

The audience knows something the character doesn't. This gap in knowledge creates deep emotional investment.

🎪

Dramatic Suspense

The slow burn of knowing something is coming. Keeps readers/viewers on edge throughout the narrative.

🔮

Point of Attack

Where the story begins — the moment of greatest conflict or interest. Everything builds from this entry point.

Inciting Incident / Catalyst — The event that sets the plot in motion. It represents the central conflict of the story and should be shown through character actions and clear narrative purpose.

02 — Concept

Conflict

Conflict is what drives narrative forward. It is gradual movements that involve emotional change in characters — sudden sharp charges that reveal personality, vulnerability, and growth. All conflict leads to a conclusion.

Types of Conflict

⚔️

Cause & Effect

Actions ripple through the narrative, creating consequences the character must face

🧠

Internal

The character's inner struggle — moral dilemmas, fear, self-doubt, hidden desires

🌍

External

Character vs. society, environment, another person, or supernatural forces

⏳

Tragic Flaw

An innate weakness or quality of character that eventually leads to their downfall

Origin of Action — Action is an expression of the character's nature shaped by various forces and conditions. A character who stands alone with no motivation is equally a strong and compelling contradiction.

Story Structure through Conflict

📖

Plays through Climaxes

Stories develop through alternating climaxes — ensuring the narrative establishes and maintains flow through scenes and chapters.

🎬

Chapter Summary

Obligatory scene: the same chapter plays out — yet there is always something to explore in the next moment of conflict.

03 — Concept

Premise

A Premise is a short, clear statement that expresses what the story is really about. It is not just the plot, but the underlying truth it is proving — a proposition stated or assumed as a basis of argument leading to a conclusion.

What Premise Does

Expresses the core idea. Stated or proved. Acts as the spine of the entire narrative — every scene should connect back to it.

Example Premises

"Greed leads to self-destruction."

"Blind ambition destroys friendship."

"Love conquers all."

Good Authorship — Authors ensure that every climax and scene does not put unnecessary emphasis on the primary. The focus should remain on the premise — never shading away from it.

04 — Concept

Character

Characterisation is the organisation of the character's personality. Good characterisation portrays and conveys character through conflicts and challenges — showing how they think, feel, and evolve. Characters must be consistently portrayed.

Type Definition Role in Story
Static Character Does not change. Remains the same throughout the narrative. Often a supporting figure. Their consistency highlights change in others.
Dynamic Character Undergoes significant internal or external change over the course of the story. Usually the protagonist. Their journey IS the story.
Protagonist The central character the audience follows. Faces the core conflict; embodies the premise.
Antagonist The opposing force — not necessarily evil, just oppositional. Creates conflict. An antithesis: a person who follows one thing but believes another.

Character Growth — Characters grow through conflicts and choices. They may involve complex types — combining static and dynamic qualities — introducing slowly increasing conflict that motivates development and disclosure of their true nature.

🎭

Action & Motivation

Actions arise from the character's nature. Their past, fears, desires, and beliefs all shape the choices they make under pressure.

⚖️

Conflict → Development

The antagonist's actions lead to the character's development. Conflict is the furnace in which character is forged.

05 — Concept

Plot

Plot vs Character — Plot is a chain of events, but it is always the character who drives it. Characters plotting their own story define narrative scenarios and actions. The most important thing is that characters transcend the story's premise.

Pivotal Characters — Protagonist or antagonist propels the narrative forward. They are fundamentally bound to establish the central conflict. They are equally strong and compelling — creating a story arc that is genuinely satisfying.

Plot & Character Relationship

📌

Plot defines Character

Events reveal who someone truly is under pressure

🧩

Character defines Plot

A character's choices and desires shape the direction of events

🔄

Both are inseparable

Great stories weave character and plot so tightly they become one

06 — Concept

Environment

Environment influences characters in widespread and sometimes unexpected ways. Social factors, personality, and upbringing — even political and economic factors — all shape a character's individual perspective and decisions.

🌏

Social Factors

Class, culture, community pressure, and relationships all influence how a character thinks and behaves in key moments.

🏛️

Political & Economic

The world's systems — poverty, power, war — create the external conditions that force characters into impossible choices.

🎓

Upbringing & Origin

Where a character comes from shapes their worldview. Their origin story is part of their present identity and future arc.

🧬

Individual Perspective

Each character filters the world uniquely. Two people in the same environment can have completely different reactions.

07 — Case Study

Example: Spider-Man

Spider-Man is the perfect illustration of all storytelling principles working together. Every concept from these notes is present in this one story.

Act I — Foreshadowing

Getting Powers

Peter Parker is bitten by a radioactive spider. The premise is set — with great power comes great responsibility — but he doesn't know it yet. This is the inciting incident.

Act II — Conflict

Uncle Ben's Death

Peter's choice to do nothing triggers the greatest tragedy of his life. This is cause and effect in its most devastating form — his inaction becomes the engine of the story.

Act III — Resolution

The Final Fight

Everything converges. Character, conflict, environment, and premise all collide in a climax that proves the story's central truth.

With great power comes great responsibility.
— Spider-Man / The Premise of the Story

This single line is the premise. Every event — the spider bite, Uncle Ben's death, the final fight — all lead to proving this one statement. That is how foreshadowing, conflict, character, plot, and environment work in perfect harmony.

"Only those people who are familiar with mortality feel the desire to leave a mark. I feel death is a small price to pay for immortality."
